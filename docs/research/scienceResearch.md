## KEY FINDINGS
- Mixing formats wins: meta-analytic evidence (Adesope 2017) shows MCQ practice is at least as potent as free recall (g=0.70 vs 0.62) and mixed formats are strongest, so the app should pair its recall bank with an exam-format MCQ mode rather than choose between them.
- Fixed 1/2/4/7/14d interval ladders are scientifically defensible (expanding vs equal spacing shows no reliable difference; 14d is within Cepeda's optimal 10-20% of a 90-day horizon), but the queue must become response-contingent: lapses reset, and items should graduate only after 3 correct recalls across 3 spaced sessions per Rawson & Dunlosky's successive-relearning criterion.
- FSRS beats SM-2 in ~99.6% of collections on a 727M-review benchmark with ~20-30% fewer reviews for equal retention, and its desired-retention parameter (default 0.90) is the right abstraction if the app later upgrades its scheduler.
- Interleaving across domains yields a moderate benefit (g=0.42) driven by discriminative contrast, so the daily queue should shuffle domains and include explicit confusable-pair discrimination items (e.g., LoRA vs P-tuning, Triton vs TensorRT-LLM).
- High-confidence errors are the most correctable (hypercorrection effect) but relapse within a week, so the app should capture per-item confidence before answer reveal, show a confident-but-wrong danger-zone quadrant on the dashboard, and re-test those items at both ~2 and ~7 days.
- Two to three full-length timed mocks (at ~T-21/T-10/T-4 days) are sufficient for readiness prediction; real scores track the median of recent attempts, so show a median-of-last-two readiness band, never a single-point prediction.
- Elaborated feedback beats correct-answer-only feedback, so every MCQ needs per-distractor rationales (UWorld model), a one-sentence self-explanation gate after misses, and an auto-generated error log that spawns recall cards.
- The doer effect gives hands-on practice ~6x the learning impact of reading (causally replicated across 12,500+ students), validating the labs' hypothesis/reflection design, which also operationalizes the pretesting effect.
- Habit evidence favors a minimum-dose daily session (~5 items) with streak-freeze/repair tokens (48% longer streaks in apps that have them) and user-chosen implementation-intention cues; missing one day does not derail habit formation (Lally 2010), so never hard-reset streaks.

# Learning-Science Review for the NCA-GENL Study App

Scope: seven evidence areas, each with a summary and a concrete feature recommendation tailored to the app's existing components (open-ended recall bank; 1-5 self-rated review queue with fixed 1/2/4/7/14d intervals; objectives with confidence ratings; labs with hypothesis/reflection; weak-areas dashboard).

---

## 1. Retrieval practice: free recall vs recognition (MCQ)

**Evidence.** Practice testing is one of the best-supported interventions in learning science, but the format story is more nuanced than "recall always beats recognition." [Adesope, Trevisan & Sundararajan (2017)](https://journals.sagepub.com/doi/abs/10.3102/0034654316689306), a meta-analysis of 272 effects, found MCQ practice produced a *larger* mean benefit (g = 0.70) than free recall (g = 0.62) or short answer (g = 0.48) — and crucially, **a mixture of formats yielded the strongest effect**. The classic [Kang, McDermott & Roediger (2007)](https://www.researchgate.net/publication/224943432_Test_format_and_corrective_feedback_modify_the_effect_of_testing_on_long-term_retention) study found that *with corrective feedback*, short-answer practice slightly beat MCQ practice **even when the final test was multiple choice** — consistent with retrieval-effort accounts — while without feedback MCQ practice won. [McDermott et al. (2014)](https://pdf.retrievalpractice.org/guide/McDermott_etal_2014_JEPA.pdf) confirmed in real classrooms that both quiz formats enhance later exam performance regardless of final-test format. Transfer-appropriate processing still matters at the margin: some format-matched benefit exists, so an MCQ exam justifies MCQ exposure — but generative recall builds the more durable, flexible memory ([review of applied research](https://www.frontiersin.org/journals/education/articles/10.3389/feduc.2019.00005/full)). Well-built MCQs with plausible distractors approach short-answer effectiveness ([Uner, Tekin & Roediger 2021](http://psychnet.wustl.edu/memory/wp-content/uploads/2021/09/Uner-Tekin-Roediger-2021.pdf)).

**Recommendation.** Keep the open-ended recall bank as the primary durability engine, but add a parallel **NCA-GENL-format MCQ mode** (single-best-answer, 4 options, timed per-question like the real exam). Adopt a two-phase pipeline per concept: recall-first (generate the answer unaided), then MCQ (discriminate among distractors). A simple rule: every concept graduating past the 4-day interval in the recall queue unlocks 1-2 linked MCQs; final two weeks before the exam, shift the mix toward ~60% MCQ for format fluency while keeping recall reps for the weakest areas. Always show corrective feedback immediately (see #6).

---

## 2. Spaced repetition: SM-2 vs FSRS, fixed ladders, successive relearning

**Evidence.** FSRS models each card's difficulty, stability, and retrievability and fits parameters to the user's own review history; the [open srs-benchmark](https://github.com/open-spaced-repetition/srs-benchmark) (~10,000 Anki users, ~727M reviews, ~350M analyzed) shows FSRS-6 predicts recall more accurately than SM-2 in ~99.6% of collections, and simulations suggest **20-30% fewer reviews for the same retention** ([Anki FAQ](https://faqs.ankiweb.net/what-spaced-repetition-algorithm); [FSRS vs SM-2 overview](https://www.antiagent.io/blog/fsrs-vs-sm-2)). Anki made FSRS available by default from v23.10. FSRS's key UX innovation is the **desired-retention parameter** (default 0.90): the user picks a recall-probability target and the scheduler derives intervals — an ideal knob for a fixed-date cert. However, the 20-30% figure is from logged-data simulation, not an RCT. Are fixed ladders defensible? Mostly yes at cert-prep timescales: [Karpicke & Roediger (2007)](https://www.researchgate.net/publication/6261284_Expanding_Retrieval_Practice_Promotes_Short-Term_Retention_but_Equally_Spaced_Retrieval_Enhances_Long-Term_Retention) and follow-ups found expanding schedules confer no reliable advantage over equal spacing — *absolute* spacing is what matters — and [Cepeda et al. (2008)](https://laplab.ucsd.edu/articles/Cepeda%20et%20al%202008_psychsci.pdf) found the optimal gap is roughly 10-20% of the retention interval, so a 14-day cap is squarely in range for a 1-3 month prep horizon (10-20% of 90 days = 9-18 days). The bigger deficiency of a fixed ladder is not the interval values but **failing to react to lapses and per-item difficulty**. Finally, [Rawson & Dunlosky's successive-relearning research](https://journals.sagepub.com/doi/full/10.1177/09637214221100484) prescribes: recall each item to a criterion of **3 correct recalls in the initial session, then relearn to criterion in ~3 widely spaced sessions**; effect sizes vs single-session learning ranged 1.52-4.19, with ~80% recall a week later ([Rawson & Dunlosky 2013](https://www.semanticscholar.org/paper/The-Power-of-Successive-Relearning:-Improving-on-Rawson-Dunlosky/ab9fe6e3290d62086cba93ed67cea421f75eba08)).

**Recommendation.** Don't rip out the ladder for full FSRS — instead make it **response-contingent and criterion-based**: (a) a rating of 1-2 resets the item to the 1-day rung and requires re-answering correctly *within the same session* before it counts (successive relearning's "to criterion" rule); (b) a rating of 5 may skip a rung; (c) an item only "graduates" (leaves the active queue) after 3 successful recalls across 3 separate spaced sessions, not after one pass through the ladder. Add an **exam-date-aware cap**: intervals never exceed ~15% of days-remaining-to-exam, so everything gets at least one touch near exam day. If you later want more efficiency, swap in the open-source FSRS scheduler (ts-fsrs / fsrs-rs) with desired retention exposed as a single setting (default 0.90) — the 1-5 self-rating maps cleanly onto FSRS's Again/Hard/Good/Easy.

---

## 3. Interleaving vs blocking; contextual variation

**Evidence.** The [Brunmair & Richter (2019) meta-analysis](https://www.researchgate.net/publication/335004545_Similarity_matters_A_meta-analysis_of_interleaved_learning_and_its_moderators) (59 studies, 238 effects) found a moderate overall interleaving benefit (g = 0.42), driven by **discriminative contrast** — juxtaposing confusable categories forces learners to notice distinguishing features. Interleaving has also been shown to improve problem-solving in [undergraduate physics](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41539-021-00110-x). Important moderators: for simple verbal materials (word lists) blocking can win (g = -0.39 in that meta-analysis), and blocking helps early rule-discovery ([Intelligence 2023 review](https://www.mdpi.com/2079-3200/11/8/153)); the interleaving advantage holds at delayed tests, which is what matters for an exam. The motor-learning contextual-interference literature converges: random/varied practice hurts immediate performance but improves retention and transfer ([2024 systematic review](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-024-65753-3)), and moderate variation in practice conditions aids transfer without harming learning ([VR context-variability study](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pmc/articles/PMC9688041/)).

**Recommendation.** Make the daily review queue **interleaved across NCA-GENL domains by default** (shuffle so no more than 2 consecutive items share a domain), while keeping *first exposure* to a new topic blocked (a short focused intro set). Add explicit **discrimination items** targeting the exam's classic confusables — e.g., LoRA vs prompt-tuning vs P-tuning; RAG vs fine-tuning use cases; Triton Inference Server vs TensorRT-LLM vs NeMo; BLEU vs ROUGE vs perplexity — formatted as "which of these applies here and why not the others." Vary surface context on repeat exposures: re-ask the same objective through a scenario stem, a definition, and a comparison rather than the identical card each time.

## 4. Metacognition and calibration

**Evidence.** Learners' confidence is only loosely coupled to accuracy, and miscalibration is behaviorally costly: overconfident students under-prepare and stop studying too early, and calibration discrepancy predicts subsequent study-strategy choices ([IJ AIED 2025](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s40593-025-00514-5); [Frontiers pharmacy-students study](https://www.frontiersin.org/journals/psychology/articles/10.3389/fpsyg.2026.1720303/full)). Even high achievers overestimate competence while calibrating relative performance well ([Lee 2024](https://onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/10.1111/jpr.12409)). The **hypercorrection effect** is the design opportunity: errors made with *high* confidence are the most likely to be corrected after feedback, because the surprise of being wrong drives attention to the correction ([Butterfield & Metcalfe](https://www.researchgate.net/publication/11641193_Errors_Committed_with_High_Confidence_Are_Hypercorrected); [Metcalfe & Finn 2011](https://www.columbia.edu/cu/psychology/metcalfe/PDFs/MetcalfeFinn2011.pdf)) — but the correction decays: high-confidence errors tend to **return after about a week** unless re-tested ([Butler, Fazio & Marsh 2011](https://link.springer.com/article/10.3758/s13423-011-0173-y)). Prediction-then-feedback designs (judge first, then see the answer) both exploit hypercorrection and generate the calibration data.

**Recommendation.** The app already has objective-level confidence; push it to the **item level**: before revealing an answer, require a one-tap confidence rating (sure / probably / guessing). Then: (a) compute a running **calibration score** (e.g., Brier score or a confidence-vs-accuracy curve) shown on the weak-areas dashboard; (b) render the dashboard as a 2x2: *confident+correct* (mastered), *unconfident+correct* (underconfident — reassure, space out), *unconfident+wrong* (known gap — normal queue), *confident+wrong* (**danger zone** — highlight in red and schedule these for re-test at 1-2 days AND again ~7 days later, since hypercorrected items relapse); (c) before each practice exam, ask the user to predict their score, then chart predicted-vs-actual over time so calibration itself becomes a tracked skill.

## 5. Practice exams: dosing, prediction, error-log protocol

**Evidence.** Data from high-stakes exam ecosystems (MCAT/USMLE — the best-instrumented analog for a proctored MCQ cert) show **2-3 well-timed full-length practice exams are enough for a solid readiness estimate**; more full-lengths add little predictive value versus targeted question practice ([Residency Advisor analysis](https://residencyadvisor.com/resources/mcat-prep/score-prediction-accuracy-how-close-are-practice-tests-to-real-mcat)). Prediction is most accurate when the practice test is taken within ~2-4 weeks of the real exam, real scores land closer to the **median of recent attempts than the best attempt**, and irreducible day-of noise is roughly ±5 points ([Crucial Exams](https://crucialexams.com/posts/blog/practice-exam-score-vs-actual-exam-score-what-to-really-expect); [iatrox comparison](https://www.iatrox.com/blog/score-predictors-compared-how-accurate-are-they)). On review protocol, the consensus practice among top scorers (UWorld/CPA/USMLE communities) is a deliberate **error log**: for every miss, cover the options and explain in writing why the right answer is right and each wrong answer is wrong, classify the error, convert misses into flashcards, and re-attempt incorrects after a delay — deliberate review of a single Q-bank predicts scores better than raw question volume ([UWorld CPA guidance](https://accounting.uworld.com/blog/cpa-review/failing-cpa-exam-effective-review-techniques/); [Mehlman on reviewing incorrects](https://mehlmanmedical.com/how-should-i-review-my-incorrect-questions-in-uworld/)).

**Recommendation.** Build a **mock-exam module with a fixed dosing plan**: one diagnostic early (sets the weak-areas baseline), then full timed mocks at roughly T-21, T-10, and T-4 days (NCA-GENL length/timing: ~50-60 questions, 1 hour). Readiness indicator = median of the last two mocks with an explicit ±5-8% uncertainty band against the passing threshold — never show a single-point "predicted score." Every missed or guessed-right question auto-creates an **error-log entry** requiring (a) an error classification (knowledge gap / misread stem / fell for distractor / calculation-detail), and (b) a one-sentence written explanation of why the correct answer is correct; the entry then spawns a recall-bank card injected into the spaced queue. Guessed-correct items (low confidence + correct) get logged too — they are hidden gaps.

## 6. Elaboration: distractor rationales, self-explanation, generation, labs

**Evidence.** Feedback meta-analyses consistently rank **elaborated feedback > knowledge-of-correct-response > right/wrong only** for learning outcomes ([Van der Kleij et al. meta-analysis](https://www.researchgate.net/publication/272923307_Effects_of_Feedback_in_a_Computer-Based_Learning_Environment_on_Students'_Learning_Outcomes_A_Meta-Analysis); a [2026 meta-analysis of 51 studies](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s10648-026-10117-8) further shows feedback amplifies MCQ testing benefits and neutralizes the risk that plausible distractors seed misconceptions). This is precisely the UWorld model: rationales for the correct answer *and each distractor*, addressing the specific misconception each distractor encodes ([UWorld review](https://www.exam-labs.com/blog/uworld-nclex-review-benefits-drawbacks-my-scores-and-expert-tips)). Self-explanation prompting reliably improves conceptual and procedural learning across domains, with explanation *quality* predicting gains ([Bisra et al. / instruction review](https://education.asu.edu/sites/g/files/litvpz656/files/lcl/instruction_based_on_self_explanation.pdf); [self-explanation of video lectures](https://educationaltechnologyjournal.springeropen.com/articles/10.1186/s41239-023-00383-9)). Student **question generation** promotes learning and metacognitive monitoring, especially when paired with student-written explanations ([Interactive Learning Environments](https://www.tandfonline.com/doi/abs/10.1080/10494820.2019.1686398)). And the **doer effect** is the strongest card for labs: doing practice has ~6x the effect on learning outcomes of reading alone, with causal (not just correlational) analyses replicated across 7+ courses and 12,500+ students ([Koedinger et al. via LAK](https://dl.acm.org/doi/10.1145/2883851.2883957); [doer effect at scale](https://dl.acm.org/doi/pdf/10.1145/3576050.3576103)). Pretesting/errorful-generation research adds that attempting before knowing (the labs' hypothesis step) improves subsequent learning even when the attempt fails ([Pan & Sana 2021](https://www.researchgate.net/publication/348155988_Pan_and_Sana_2021_Pretesting_vs_posttesting_Errorful_generation_prequestions_and_retrieval_practice)).

**Recommendation.** Four concrete moves: (1) Author every MCQ with **per-distractor rationales** — a schema field per option stating the misconception it represents; show all four rationales after answering, not just the correct one. (2) After any miss, add a 15-second **self-explanation gate**: "In one sentence, why is your chosen answer wrong?" before the next question (skippable but tracked). (3) Add a weekly **"write 2 exam questions"** task auto-targeted at the two weakest dashboard domains — user writes stem + 4 options + rationales; these enter their personal bank and get quizzed later (generation + retrieval in one loop). (4) Keep the labs' hypothesis/reflection structure exactly as is — it operationalizes pretesting + self-explanation — but link each lab reflection to specific exam objectives so completing a lab bumps those objectives' evidence in the dashboard.

## 7. Habit design: streaks, session goals, minimum dose

**Evidence.** [Lally et al. (2010)](https://onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/10.1002/ejsp.674) found real-world habit automaticity takes a median **66 days** (range 18-254) and — critically — **missing a single day did not materially derail** habit formation; rigid never-miss framing is not evidence-based. Streak mechanics do work behaviorally, but mainly via loss aversion, and they backfire when a break resets everything to zero: apps with a **streak-freeze** mechanic see users average 48% longer streaks (17.2 vs 11.6 days) than apps without ([Trophy gamification case study](https://trophy.so/blog/duolingo-gamification-case-study)), and Duolingo's own data ties streak commitment devices to ~14% D14 retention lifts ([streak psychology breakdown](https://www.justanotherpm.com/blog/the-psychology-behind-duolingos-streak-feature)). The durable driver is a tiny **minimum viable daily session** anchored to a consistent cue (implementation intentions), with identity ("I'm someone who reviews daily") outlasting loss aversion ([Professor Game on streak backfire](https://www.professorgame.com/podcast/423/)). Note this whole area rests on weaker, largely industry evidence than sections 1-6 — treat as sensible defaults, not settled science.

**Recommendation.** Implement a **minimum-dose streak**: the day counts if the user completes just the due review queue or 5 items (~3 minutes) — labs and mocks are bonuses, never the bar. Grant an automatic **streak repair** (one per 7 active days, banked to max 2) so a single missed day never zeroes the counter, matching the Lally finding. Add a one-time **implementation-intention setup** ("When/where will you study? e.g., 'after morning coffee'") with the reminder notification tied to that self-chosen cue rather than a generic time. Frame the weekly goal as sessions/week (e.g., 6 of 7) instead of a perfection streak, and surface days-to-exam plus queue-size forecast ("12 min/day keeps you on pace") so effort feels bounded. Avoid XP inflation, leagues, and cosmetic badges — for a motivated adult cert candidate they add noise; the streak + visible mastery progress (graduated items, calibration trend) carries the load.

## Cross-cutting synthesis for this app

The app's existing skeleton is well aligned with the evidence; the highest-leverage additions, in order: (1) exam-format MCQ bank with per-distractor rationales and an auto-populating error log (sections 1, 5, 6); (2) criterion-based graduation and lapse-handling in the review queue — successive relearning semantics on top of the existing ladder (section 2); (3) item-level confidence capture feeding a calibration quadrant on the weak-areas dashboard with fast re-testing of confident errors (section 4); (4) interleaved queue ordering plus confusable-pair discrimination items (section 3); (5) minimum-dose streak with repair tokens (section 7). None of these require replacing existing components — they extend the recall bank, queue, confidence tracking, labs, and dashboard already in place.
